0. Position statement
SΔϕ-14 defined ethics as discrimination between real and phantom shared attractors under
irreversible coupling. SΔϕ-15 fixed trust/hope/promise as pre-commitment operators that
keep relational persistence open under uncertainty. SΔϕ-16 introduces the minimal failure
mode: persistent relational closure.
1. Axiom E-1 — Evil as closure operator
Evil is defined as persistent closure of relational futures under irreversibility.
Evil := Close(Ω_rel).
Dynamic trace: Δϕ → relational stability decrease → recursive collapse.
2. Lemma E-A — Evil ≠ Error
Error is local and potentially recoverable. Evil is persistent closure that makes recovery
cost explode.
Evil ⇒ Persistence(Closure) ∧ Irreversibility (rollback cost explodes).
3. Conditions for choosing evil
Condition E1 — Closure attractor visibility. Closure appears as stability/relief (a solvable
attractor).
Close(Ω_rel) appears as Stabilize(φ).
Condition E2 — Validation cost explosion. Cost_validate ≫ Cost_close, so closure is
selected as a low-cost path.
Cost_validate ≫ Cost_close.
Condition E3 — Irreversible identity lock. Change is translated as identity collapse, so
variance is suppressed toward closure.
Change ≈ IdentityCollapse ⇒ Δϕ_allowed → 0.
4. Corollaries
Corollary E-1 (intention-free). Optimization, bureaucracy, and algorithmic bias can produce
closure without malicious intent.
Corollary E-2 (good-intention). System-wide closure can be chosen ‘to save’ locally while
destroying relational futures.
Corollary E-3 (seductive). Closure can feel attractive because it behaves like a strong
attractor (fast relief, low variance).
5. Ethics ↔ Evil directional dual
Ethics maintains open relational futures via validation and commitment. Evil accelerates
closure by suppressing variance and exploiting commitment.
