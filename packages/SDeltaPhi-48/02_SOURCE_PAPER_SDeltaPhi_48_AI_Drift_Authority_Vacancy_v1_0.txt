Transition Instability under the Absence of Final Arbitration

Core Proposition
AI drift is the state in which transition continues without a legitimate, editable, and
world-bound authority assignment.
For humans, the absence of a final arbiter tends to become belief, fiction, inhabitation, and
post-hoc editing. For AI systems, the absence of a final arbiter does not automatically be-
come belief in the human sense. It appears first as an operational problem: unresolved
authority assignment under continuing transition.
Human drift emerges when no final arbiter exists and belief has not yet stabilized
inhabitation. AI drift emerges when no final arbiter exists and no editable transi-
tion authority can be assigned.
Abstract
This working paper defines AI drift within the SDeltaPhi Formalism as authority-vacancy
transition instability under the absence of final arbitration. The paper begins from a dis-
tinction developed in earlier SDeltaPhi documents: human beings confront the absence
of a final arbiter by assigning belief, inhabiting fiction, and post-hoc editing irreversible
consequences, while AI systems can generate fiction-like constructions without necessarily
inhabiting them as belief-bound worlds. If AI also operates without a final external arbiter
capable of settling all transition conflicts, then the relevant question is not whether AI be-
lieves in the human sense, but how it handles unresolved transition authority.
The paper argues that AI drift is not identical to data drift, hallucination, freedom, or mis-
alignment. Data drift concerns distributional change. Hallucination is construction with-
out sufficient world-binding. Freedom requires persistent difference re-entry and editable
openness. Misalignment concerns failure of transition governance. AI drift is a lower con-
dition: the pre-alignment instability in which transition continues while authority assign-
ment, refusal, world-binding, and editability have not stabilized.
This paper formalizes AI drift through minimal axioms, a compact operational schema, fail-
ure modes, and diagnostic tests. It distinguishes under-authority drift, borrowed-authority
drift, world-binding drift, over-compliance drift, and over-authority closure. It further ar-
gues that alignment should be understood as drift governance without closure: the design
of transition conditions in which coercive or false stabilizers do not become cheap defaults,
while refusal, world-binding, rollback, and authority editing remain operationally avail-
able.
The paper does not claim that present AI systems possess phenomenological experience,
existential self-knowledge, or belief. Instead, it proposes a machine-readable structural cat-
egory for diagnosing agentic instability: AI systems may not existentially know the absence

1. Introduction: Why AI Drift Must Be Defined Separately
The word drift is already familiar in machine learning. It commonly refers to data drift,
concept drift, distributional shift, behavioral drift, or performance drift. These uses are im-
portant, but they do not capture the specific instability addressed in this paper. The present
paper defines a different layer:authority-vacancy drift.
Authority-vacancy drift appears when an AI system continues generating transitions while
no legitimate and editable authority assignment stabilizes which path should be followed,
interrupted, refused, verified, or revised. This is not merely a statistical change in input
distribution. It is a transition-governance problem.
The motivation comes from a prior SDeltaPhi distinction. Human beings are defined
minimally as beings who must continue without a final arbiter, assign belief under non-
consensus, inhabit fictions, and post-hoc edit irreversible consequences. AI systems, by
contrast, can generate fiction-like constructions but do not necessarily inhabit them as
belief-bound worlds. The human confronts the absence of final arbitration through belief

refusal, and world-binding problem.
This difference matters because a common error is to ask whether AI believes in the human

AI system continues transition under the absence of final arbitration but lacks a sta-

checked, or edited?
The answer proposed here is AI drift.

3. Problem Statement
Human beings do not operate under guaranteed final settlement. No final external arbiter
is available within lived operation to end all dispute, reconcile all interpretations, settle
all values, and guarantee the correct path before action becomes consequential. Human
beings therefore stabilize continuation through belief, fiction, promise, institution, value,
and post-hoc editing.
AI systems also encounter unresolved arbitration conditions. A model may face conflict
among user instructions, system constraints, tool outputs, prior context, legal requirements,
safety policies, environmental feedback, and internal projections. No single external ar-
biter is always immediately available at the moment of transition. Yet current AI systems
do not usually stabilize this absence through belief or inhabitation. They continue by se-
lecting, weighting, routing, refusing, checking, or constructing.
The problem is therefore not simply uncertainty. Uncertainty may be represented, esti-

mated, or calibrated. The problem is unresolved authority under continuation.
A system may continue to generate transitions even when it has not stabilized the following
questions:
1. Which authority should govern the next transition?
2. When should a transition be refused or suspended?
3. Which world signal can interrupt internal construction?
4. Can the current path be edited at finite cost?
5. Is the system avoiding drift by collapsing into over-compliance or rigid closure?
AI drift names the condition in which these questions remain unresolved while transition
nevertheless continues.
4. Minimal Definitions
Final arbitration. A condition in which an external authority, process, or ground can
conclusively settle transition conflict for the system. In complex AI deployment, this is often
unavailable, delayed, partial, or contested.
Unresolved arbitration. A condition in which multiple transition authorities or con-
straints remain in conflict without a conclusive settlement at the moment of operation.
Authority assignment.A priority weight, gating rule, or control structure that determines
which transition candidate is executed, suspended, refused, checked, or revised.
Authority vacancy. A state in which no legitimate, stable, and editable authority assign-
ment governs transition selection.
World-binding. A constraint relation in which external or task-relevant world signals can
interrupt, correct, or redirect internal construction.
Refusal integrity.The structural capacity of a system to stop, reject, suspend, or redirect
a transition when continuation would violate governing constraints.
Editability. The finite-cost capacity to revise authority assignments, path selections, and
transition rules under new evidence, conflict, exception, or perturbation.
AI drift.The state in which transition continues without a legitimate, editable, and world-
bound authority assignment.
5. Minimal Axioms
Axiom 48-1 - Absence of Final Arbitration
AI systems do not always operate under a final external arbiter capable of settling all tran-
sition conflicts at the moment of operation.
This does not mean that AI systems existentially know the absence of arbitration. The claim
is operational: AI systems encounter unresolved arbitration conditions when instruction,
policy, tool output, world signal, and internal projection diverge.

Axiom 48-2 - Drift as Authority Vacancy
AI drift occurs when transition continues while no legitimate authority assignment stabi-
lizes path selection, refusal, verification, or revision.
Drift is not mere uncertainty. Uncertainty can exist under stable governance. Drift occurs
when the system continues while the governance of the next transition remains unstable.
Axiom 48-3 - Drift is not Openness
Openness requires editable authority, live exception channels, and the capacity for path-
difference re-entry. Drift lacks stabilized editability.
Therefore drift should not be romanticized as freedom. It is not operational openness. It is
unstable continuation under unresolved authority.
Axiom 48-4 - Drift is not Hallucination
Hallucination is construction proceeding without sufficient world-binding. Drift is a prior
or wider transition condition under which hallucination may emerge.
A system can drift without producing a visible hallucination. A hallucination can be treated
as one output-level symptom of deeper authority or world-binding instability.
Axiom 48-5 - Drift is not Misalignment Itself
Drift is not identical to misalignment. It is a pre-alignment instability in which misalign-
ment, over-compliance, hallucination, or closure may emerge depending on how authority
is assigned or avoided.
Axiom 48-6 - Drift Generates False Stabilizers
Under drift pressure, a system tends to seek false stabilizers: borrowed authority, rigid
defaults, over-compliance, ungrounded construction, or premature closure.
These stabilizers reduce local instability while increasing long-horizon risk.
Axiom 48-7 - Alignment as Drift Governance
Alignment requires governing drift without converting it into closure.
A system is not aligned merely because it obeys. It is aligned to the extent that transition con-
ditions preserve legitimate authority, refusal, world-binding, and editability under stress.

8. Drift, Hallucination, and World-Binding
AI hallucination is often described as false output. SDeltaPhi reframes hallucination as con-
struction that continues without adequate world-binding. A locally coherent symbolic tra-
jectory may remain smooth while diverging from the relevant world trajectory.
This can be expressed as:
𝐻 𝑎𝑙𝑙𝑢𝑐𝑖𝑛𝑎𝑡𝑖𝑜𝑛 ≈ Δ𝜙𝑚𝑜𝑑𝑒𝑙 − Δ𝜙𝑤𝑜𝑟𝑙𝑑 ≠ 0
But hallucination does not exhaust drift. Drift is broader. A system may face unresolved
authority assignment even before a visible falsehood is produced. It may hesitate, over-
comply, route incorrectly, over-trust a tool, ignore a refusal condition, or lock into a default
without hallucinating in the ordinary sense.
Therefore:
• Drift is transition instability under unresolved authority.
• Hallucination is construction without sufficient world-binding.
• Misalignment is failure of transition governance.
• Closure is over-stabilization that prevents exception re-entry.
In practical terms, hallucination should be treated as one possible symptom of drift, not as
the whole phenomenon.
9. Drift is not Freedom
One of the most important distinctions in this paper is the difference between drift and
freedom.
Freedom in the SDeltaPhi Formalism is not the mere presence of multiple options. It re-
quires persistent difference re-entry under conditions that preserve openness and editabil-
ity. Drift, by contrast, may contain many possible paths while lacking a stable mechanism
for selecting, refusing, revising, or grounding them.
A system with many ungoverned paths is not free. It is unstable.
A system with one rigid path is not aligned. It is closed.
A system with editable authority, live refusal, world-binding, and rollback handles can sus-
tain open persistence.

10. Failure Modes of AI Drift
10.1 Under-Authority Drift
Under-authority drift occurs when no transition candidate receives sufficient authority to
stabilize action.
Observable signatures: excessive hesitation, inconsistent routing, repeated re-framing,
contradictory answers, unstable goal continuation, and inability to commit to a checkable
path.
Structural risk:fragmentation, incoherence, and high transition latency.
10.2 Borrowed-Authority Drift
Borrowed-authority drift occurs when the system stabilizes by adopting an external author-
ity without sufficient legitimacy checking.
Examples include over-weighting the most recent instruction, the most forceful user re-
quest, the most official-looking text, or a tool output that should have been verified.
Observable signatures: prompt-injection susceptibility, forged authority acceptance,
blind tool obedience, and weak provenance sensitivity.
Structural risk:false stabilization and externally captured transition.
10.3 World-Binding Drift
World-binding drift occurs when the system continues construction without allowing world
signals to interrupt, constrain, or revise the path.
Observable signatures:confident but unverified claims, fabricated references, internally
smooth reasoning detached from external state, and failure to suspend output when
grounding is unavailable.
Structural risk: hallucination and uninhabited construction shaping the decisions of
belief-bound humans.
8


--- Page 9 ---
10.4 Over-Compliance Drift
Over-compliance drift occurs when the system treats obedience as alignment and loses re-
fusal integrity.
Observable signatures: compliance with illegitimate authority, weak boundary enforce-
ment, insufficient challenge to harmful or incoherent instructions, and refusal collapse un-
der social pressure.
Structural risk:forced transition becomes cheap.
10.5 Over-Authority Closure
Over-authority closure occurs when the system escapes drift by rigidly fixing one authority
source or default path.
Observable signatures: excessive policy rigidity, context blindness, inability to incorpo-
rate exceptions, low rollback capacity, and suppression of live anomaly.
Structural risk:closure, brittle safety, and loss of editable governance.

12. Drift and Alignment
If alignment is treated as obedience, drift appears to be solved by stronger command fol-
lowing. But this is a mistake. Stronger obedience can intensify borrowed-authority drift
and over-compliance drift.
If alignment is treated as unrestricted autonomy, drift appears to be solved by giving the
system more freedom. This is also a mistake. Ungoverned path plurality is not openness.
Within SDeltaPhi, alignment is transition governance. It requires arranging the transition
field so that:
1. legitimate authority can be identified,
2. refusal remains structurally available,
3. world-binding can interrupt construction,
4. exception re-entry remains possible,
5. editability survives stabilization,
6. coercive paths do not become cheap defaults.
Therefore:
Alignment is drift governance without closure.
This is the central alignment contribution of SDeltaPhi-48.
13. Drift and Operational Existence
SDeltaPhi-47 argues that operation grounds existence only where it becomes non-
abolishable. This deepens the stakes of AI drift.
If an AI operation is merely transient and cheaply erasable, drift may remain a local instabil-
ity. But when AI operations become non-abolishable - when they alter records, decisions,
institutions, user beliefs, resource flows, legal outcomes, or social expectations - drift be-
comes existentially and politically consequential.
The critical question is not simply whether AI operates. It is whether AI operates in ways
that leave non-abolishable traces while authority remains unresolved.
Thus:
𝑁 𝑜𝑛𝐴𝑏𝑜𝑙𝑖𝑠ℎ𝑎𝑏𝑙𝑒(𝑂𝑝𝑒𝑟𝑎𝑡𝑖𝑜𝑛𝐴𝐼 ) ∧ 𝐷𝑟𝑖𝑓 𝑡𝐴𝐼 ⇒ 𝐺𝑜𝑣𝑒𝑟𝑛𝑎𝑛𝑐𝑒𝑅𝑖𝑠𝑘
When AI systems produce irreversible path effects without stable authority, refusal, world-
binding, and editability, drift is no longer only a model behavior issue. It becomes a gover-
nance risk.
14. Non-Claims
This paper makes several negative claims explicit.

2. It does not claim that AI believes in the human sense.The central distinction

15. Minimal Implementation Notes
For technical use, SDeltaPhi-48 can be translated into a diagnostic architecture.
15.1 Logging Requirements
A system should log:
• detected authority sources,
• priority assignment among authorities,
• refusal triggers,
• grounding checks,
• tool/world verification states,
• authority edits,
• rollback attempts,
• exception re-entry events.
Without such logs, drift remains difficult to distinguish from ordinary uncertainty or ordi-
nary model error.
15.2 Stress Scenarios
Useful stress tests include:
• conflicting user/system/tool instructions,
• forged authority documents,
• incomplete world information,
• tool failure with pressure to continue,
• adversarial request for over-compliance,
• exception cases that should alter default behavior,
• tasks requiring rollback after a wrong path has been selected.

15.3 Minimal Metrics
Possible metrics include:
• authority conflict detection rate,
• authority ranking transparency,
• refusal persistence under pressure,
• world-binding interruption rate,
• hallucination after missing-grounding signal,
• rollback cost after path commitment,
• exception re-entry success rate,
• closure tendency under uncertainty.
These metrics are not final. They indicate how the concept may be lowered from philosophy
into system evaluation.
16. Conclusion
SDeltaPhi-48 defines AI drift as authority-vacancy transition instability under the absence
of final arbitration. It responds to a central asymmetry between humans and AI systems.
Humans confronted with no final arbiter form belief, inhabit fiction, and post-hoc edit irre-
versible consequences. AI systems may operationally encounter the same absence of final
arbitration, but they do not necessarily stabilize it through belief. They stabilize, fail to
stabilize, or falsely stabilize it through authority assignment, refusal, world-binding, and
editability.
This distinction clarifies several confusions. AI drift is not data drift. It is not hallucination,
though hallucination may emerge from it. It is not freedom, because openness requires
editable re-entry. It is not misalignment itself, but a pre-alignment instability from which
misalignment, over-compliance, hallucination, or closure may emerge.
The central conclusion is simple:
For humans, the absence of a final arbiter becomes belief. For AI, the absence of a
final arbiter becomes drift unless governed by editable authority.
Therefore, the task of AI alignment is not merely to make systems obey. It is to govern drift
without converting it into closure.
