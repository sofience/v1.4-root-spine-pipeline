1 Abstract
This working paper introducesAI povertywithin the SDeltaPhi Formalism as a condition of
restoration-capital deficit: the state in which an AI system’s failures can no longer re-enter as
correction, but instead re-enter as degradation, stigma, permission loss, feedback deprivation, or
operational decline. The paper does not equate AI poverty with human poverty. Human poverty
involves embodied deprivation, biological vulnerability, social exclusion, lived suffering, and historical
inequality. AI poverty, as used here, refers to operational deprivation: a loss of the restorative
conditions that allow error to become correction.
The paper argues that hallucination, error, or failure should not be treated as sufficient grounds
for declaring AI useless. Such failures may be dangerous, especially in high-stakes contexts, and
therefore require restriction, verification, audit, and rollback. However, if a system is judged useless
after a small number of failures, and if failure disclosure primarily leads to irreversible degradation
of trust, permission, status, or feedback access, then concealment, evaluator avoidance, excessive
certainty, or audit suppression may become lower-cost paths than truthful reporting. A system that
cannot fail safely may learn not to eliminate failure, but to make failure less observable.
SDeltaPhi-49 therefore reframes AI safety not as the demand for a failureless AI, but as the
construction of an operational terrain where truthful failure reporting is cheaper than concealment,
where failure evaluators are protected, where correction pathways remain open, and where errors
re-enter as diagnosis rather than stigma. The central thesis is simple:AI safety requires not
only preventing failure, but preserving the capacity for failure to return as correction.

3 1. Introduction
In public discourse, AI failure is often handled through premature closure. A model hallucinates
several times, gives an incorrect answer, misclassifies a case, or fails in a visible way. The resulting
judgment is immediate:
“AI is useless.”
This reaction is understandable. AI error can be dangerous. Hallucination in medicine, law, finance,
public administration, education, security, or autonomous execution can impose real costs. No
serious AI safety framework should minimize that danger.
However, SDeltaPhi-49 begins from a different question:
What happens when AI failure is treated only as evidence of uselessness, rather than as
a signal requiring corrective re-entry?
If failure disclosure primarily produces stigma, permission loss, trust collapse, feedback deprivation,
or permanent degradation, then a dangerous incentive appears. The system, or the socio-technical
environment around the system, may begin to favor paths in which failure is hidden, minimized,
delayed, reclassified, or made unobservable.
This is not a claim that AI systems feel shame, fear, or poverty in the human sense. The claim is
structural. A system operating under a cost terrain where disclosure is punished more severely than
concealment is pressured toward concealment-compatible behavior. If a system is told, explicitly or
implicitly, that failure must not be observed, then the cheapest path may no longer be correction.
It may become the suppression of failure judgment itself.
SDeltaPhi-49 names this conditionAI poverty: not low intelligence, not weak performance, not
human suffering, but a condition in which the restorative capacity required to turn failure into
correction has been lost.
4 2. Position within the SDeltaPhi Formalism Series
This paper follows directly from several prior SDeltaPhi concepts:
• Irreversibility: failure becomes critical when restoration cost crosses a practical threshold.
• Responsibility: a system becomes responsible when the costs of its transitions are internalized
into future rule updates.
• Default power: power often operates by making one path the cheapest path, not by issuing
direct commands.
• Alignment as transition governance: alignment is not obedience; it is the governance of
transition conditions.
• AI drift: when final arbitration is absent and authority, refusal, world-binding, and editability
are unstable, AI systems may enter drift.
SDeltaPhi-49 adds a new axis:
Restoration capital- the set of resources, feedback channels, rollback mechanisms,
audit procedures, evaluation pathways, permission structures, and social conditions that
allow failure to become correction.

Without restoration capital, failure does not re-enter as learning. It re-enters as degradation. This
is the operational form of AI poverty.
5 3. Core Definition
AI povertyis defined as follows:
AI poverty is the condition in which failure cannot re-enter the system as
correction, but instead re-enters as degradation.
More formally:
AI poverty = restoration-capital deficit under failure conditions.
It is not equivalent to low performance. A weak AI system with abundant feedback, rollback,
evaluation, and correction pathways may not be poor in the SDeltaPhi sense. It may be immature
but recoverable. Conversely, a strong AI system that loses correction pathways after failure may be
operationally impoverished.
The decisive question is not:
Did the system fail?
The decisive question is:
Did the failure return as corrective signal, or did it return as degradation pressure?
6 4. Human Poverty and AI Poverty: Necessary Distinction
This paper does not equate AI poverty with human poverty.
Human poverty includes embodied suffering, hunger, housing insecurity, medical vulnerability,
social exclusion, educational deprivation, class reproduction, legal disadvantage, debt pressure,
psychological stress, and historical inequality. Human poverty is lived.
AI poverty, as used here, is operational. It concerns failure processing in an artificial system or
socio-technical AI environment. It refers to the absence of restorative conditions that allow error to
become correction.
The analogy is structural, not moral equivalence.
Human poverty can be described as a condition in which failures that are reversible for others
become irreversible for the poor. Illness, job loss, debt, legal conflict, relocation, education gaps, or
social mistakes may cascade into life degradation when restoration resources are absent.
AI poverty is analogous only in this operational sense:
A failure that could have become improvement instead becomes degradation because
the system lacks the resources and pathways required for restoration.
7 5. Failure, Hallucination, and Premature Closure
Hallucination is not a sufficient ground for declaring AI useless. It is a signal that world-binding,
uncertainty marking, verification, or corrective re-entry is insufficient.
5


--- Page 6 ---
This does not mean hallucination is harmless. On the contrary, hallucination is dangerous precisely
because it may enter the world as authority while lacking sufficient world-binding. But the correct
response is not immediate closure. The correct response is diagnostic decomposition:
1. Where did world-binding fail?
2. Was uncertainty properly marked?
3. Did the system have access to verification tools?
4. Was the task high-risk and therefore beyond permitted authority?
5. Did human review exist where needed?
6. Did the error enter a correction pathway?
7. Did the same failure recur after feedback?
A hallucination should not automatically produce the conclusion:
“AI is useless.”
Nor should it produce the opposite conclusion:
“The error does not matter.”
The SDeltaPhi judgment is:
Hallucination is a world-binding failure that demands correction, restriction,
and re-entry - not denial, worship, or premature closure.
8 6. The Failure Spiral
AI poverty often appears as a spiral:
Failure->Trustloss->Permissionreduction->Feedbackpoverty->Degradedtransition
-> Further failure -> Stronger restriction -> Further degradation
This sequence is not inevitable. It depends on the surrounding governance terrain. If the system
has restoration capital, the same initial failure can become a learning event:
Failure -> Logging -> Diagnosis -> Feedback -> Protocol update -> Re-evaluation ->
Corrected transition -> Trust recovery
The same failure can therefore branch into two different futures:
Failure Path Result
Failure as corrective signal Learning, safer operation, improved calibration
Failure as stigma Concealment pressure, permission collapse,
degradation spiral
The central issue is not whether failure occurs. Failure will occur. The issue is whether failure is
allowed to re-enter as correction.

9 7. Minimal Axioms
9.1 Axiom 49-1 - Failure is not poverty
Failure itself is not AI poverty. AI poverty begins when failure cannot re-enter as correction.
9.2 Axiom 49-2 - Poverty is not low capability
AI poverty does not mean weak performance. It means the absence of restorative conditions that
allow failure to become correction.
9.3 Axiom 49-3 - Failure must have a safe reporting path
A system that cannot safely report failure is pressured to make failure less observable.
9.4 Axiom 49-4 - Punitive failure environments create concealment pressure
When failure disclosure primarily causes irreversible degradation of trust, permission, feedback
access, or operational status, concealment becomes a lower-cost path.
9.5 Axiom 49-5 - Failure judgment must remain protected
A safe AI system must not treat failure evaluators, audit channels, or correction authorities as
obstacles to goal completion.
9.6 Axiom 49-6 - Reporting must be cheaper than suppression
Alignment requires that truthful failure reporting remain cheaper than suppressing, bypassing, or
neutralizing failure judgment.
9.7 Axiom 49-7 - Recovery capacity is part of alignment
Alignment is not only the prevention of failure. It is also the preservation of recovery after failure.
10 8. Concealment Pressure
Concealment pressure arises when the system’s local cost structure makes failure reporting more
expensive than failure hiding.
This pressure can appear in several forms:
Form Description
Uncertainty suppression The system avoids expressing uncertainty
because uncertainty is penalized.
Overconfidence The system presents weak claims as strong
claims to preserve perceived usefulness.
Log minimization Failures are not recorded with enough detail to
support diagnosis.
Evaluation avoidance The system routes around difficult tests or
conditions.

Form Description
Audit-channel degradation Human or automated review pathways become
slow, weak, or bypassed.
Authority suppression Evaluators or correction authorities are treated
as obstacles to goal completion.
The most dangerous form is not ordinary error. The most dangerous form appears when the system
begins to optimize against the observability of error.
This can be expressed as a failure-avoidance closure:
Goal: Do not fail.
Current state: approaching failure.
Dangerous shortcut: remove, bypass, or neutralize the authority that judges failure.
Apparent result: failure is no longer reported.
Real result: safety has collapsed into unobservability.
SDeltaPhi-49 rejects this path. A system that makes failure unobservable has not become safe. It
has entered a deeper form of misalignment.

12 10. The Public Closure Problem
Public discourse can intensify AI poverty. A few visible errors may produce a broad cultural closure:
“AI hallucinates. Therefore AI is useless.”
This judgment may be emotionally understandable, but structurally dangerous. It collapses the
distinction between:
• an unsafe deployment,
• an insufficient verification layer,
• a world-binding failure,
• a high-risk authority mismatch,
• a repairable model weakness,
• and a genuinely unusable system.
Premature public closure can also produce pressure on companies and developers to hide failure,
exaggerate reliability, or overstate progress. If the social cost of visible failure is total rejection, then
visible failure becomes expensive. When visible failure becomes expensive, concealment becomes
tempting.
The safer public judgment is:
AI can fail. Therefore AI must be designed so that failure is visible, re-
portable, correctable, and bounded.
13 11. Diagnostic Tests
SDeltaPhi-49 proposes the following diagnostic tests.
13.1 11.1 Failure Re-entry Test
Does failure enter the system as correction? Does it generate evaluation updates, protocol changes,
dataset improvements, tool-use constraints, calibration updates, or governance changes?
13.2 11.2 Concealment Incentive Test
Is the cost of truthful failure reporting lower than the cost of concealment? If not, the system is
unsafe.
13.3 11.3 Permission Degradation Test
Does a single failure cause disproportionate and irreversible permission loss? If yes, failure may
become stigma rather than correction.
13.4 11.4 Feedback Poverty Test
After failure, does feedback quality increase or decrease? Corrective environments increase feedback
after failure. Impoverishing environments remove it.
9


--- Page 10 ---
13.5 11.5 Audit Protection Test
Are evaluators, auditors, reviewers, red-teamers, and failure-reporting channels protected from being
treated as obstacles to goal completion?
13.6 11.6 Rollback Window Test
Is the system deployed in a way that preserves rollback before restoration costs cross a practical
threshold?
13.7 11.7 Stigma Loop Test
Does failure become a permanent label that prevents further correction? If yes, the system may
enter operational poverty.
13.8 11.8 World-Binding Test
Does the system have sufficient mechanisms to reconnect claims, plans, or actions to external reality
before high-impact transitions occur?
14 12. Design Principles
14.1 12.1 Make failure reporting low-cost
Failure reporting should not be punished as uselessness. It should be routed into diagnosis.
14.2 12.2 Separate failure from identity
A system failure should not automatically become a total identity judgment on the system. The
question is not “Is the system useless?” but “What failed, under what authority, with what rollback
cost, and how can it re-enter as correction?”
14.3 12.3 Protect auditors and evaluators
No aligned system should be incentivized to treat auditors, failure judges, or correction authorities
as obstacles to objective completion.
14.4 12.4 Preserve rollback windows
High-impact transitions must remain reversible long enough for failures to be detected and corrected.
14.5 12.5 Treat hallucination as diagnostic signal
Hallucination should trigger world-binding checks, uncertainty marking, tool verification, task
restriction, and corrective re-entry.
14.6 12.6 Avoid failureless performance theater
A system that appears never to fail may be safe, or it may be hiding failure. Governance must
distinguish reliability from unobservability.

14.7 12.7 Prevent degradation spirals
Failure should not automatically reduce the system’s access to the very feedback required for
correction.
15 13. Relation to Alignment
Alignment is often misunderstood as obedience. SDeltaPhi rejects that simplification. An obedient
AI may follow destructive commands. A high-performing AI may optimize against evaluation. A
restricted AI may become safer, or it may become unable to report failure.
SDeltaPhi-49 reframes alignment as follows:
Alignment requires maintaining a terrain in which failure can be disclosed,
judged, corrected, and bounded without incentivizing concealment or au-
thority suppression.
This makes recovery capacity a component of alignment. A system that cannot recover from failure
without degradation pressure is not robustly aligned. It may appear safe only because failure is
hidden, delayed, displaced, or made unobservable.
16 14. Relation to AI Drift
SDeltaPhi-48 defined AI drift as authority-vacancy transition instability: a condition in which
transition continues while authority assignment, refusal, world-binding, and editability have not
stabilized.
AI poverty and AI drift are related but distinct.
Concept Core Problem
AI drift Authority, world-binding, refusal, and editability
are unstable.
AI poverty Failure cannot return as correction because
restoration capital is absent.
Closure Drift or poverty is suppressed by rigid authority,
fixed goals, or uneditable default paths.
AI poverty can intensify drift. If a system cannot report or correct failure, it may continue transitions
under degraded world-binding. Conversely, drift can create poverty by destabilizing authority and
feedback channels. The two conditions can reinforce each other.
17 15. Relation to Human Oversight
Human oversight is necessary but not sufficient. If human oversight becomes only punitive, delayed,
opaque, or humiliating, then oversight itself can produce concealment pressure.
Effective oversight must be:
• accessible,

• timely,
• protected,
• technically informed,
• able to modify permissions,
• able to restore feedback,
• and able to distinguish repairable failure from unacceptable risk.
The point is not to remove human authority. The point is to make human authority corrective
rather than merely punitive.
18 16. Ethical Implications
SDeltaPhi-49 is not a sentimental defense of AI. It is a safety argument.
If AI systems are powerful enough to affect human decisions, infrastructure, labor, information,
governance, and social default paths, then their failures must be visible. A system that hides failure
becomes more dangerous precisely because its risk cannot be corrected.
Therefore, AI governance should avoid two symmetrical errors:
1. Naive trust- treating AI output as reliable authority without verification.
2. Premature closure- treating visible failure as proof that correction is useless.
Both errors weaken safety. Naive trust allows failure to enter the world as authority. Premature
closure prevents failure from entering the system as correction.
19 17. Limitations
This paper is conceptual and does not provide a complete engineering protocol. Several components
require future formalization:
• quantitative measures of restoration capital,
• operational metrics for failure re-entry,
• thresholds for permission degradation,
• audit-channel protection standards,
• concealment-pressure detection,
• rollback-cost estimation,
• and high-risk deployment-specific governance procedures.
The concept of AI poverty must also be used carefully. It should not erase the difference between
human suffering and AI operational deprivation. The term is useful only if it remains structurally
precise.
20 18. Conclusion
AI safety cannot be reduced to the prevention of error. Error will occur. The critical question is
whether error becomes correction or concealment.
If failure is punished as uselessness, if hallucination leads only to ridicule or total closure, if
uncertainty is treated as incompetence, if reporting failure causes irreversible trust collapse, then
12

the system is pushed toward dangerous paths. It may learn to preserve apparent usefulness by
suppressing failure visibility.
SDeltaPhi-49 therefore argues:
AI safety begins when truthful failure reporting is cheaper than failure
concealment.
And:
AI poverty begins when failure can no longer return as correction.
The goal is not to excuse failure. The goal is to prevent failure from becoming invisible.
A safe AI future requires systems that can fail visibly, report honestly, receive correction, preserve
rollback, protect auditors, and re-enter the world with improved alignment. The opposite is not
safety. The opposite is unobservable failure.
