# Failure Modes

## 1. Aggregate masking

Total benefit hides individual irreversible cost.

Correction: identify cost-bearing subjects and reversibility.

## 2. Subject erasure

The cost-bearing subject disappears after the calculation.

Correction: require re-entry of harmed subjects.

## 3. Minority disposability

A minority is treated as acceptable cost.

Correction: audit consent, refusal, exit, repair, and irreversibility.

## 4. Consent laundering

Coerced, desperate, uninformed, or high-cost consent is treated as voluntary.

Correction: inspect refusal and exit cost.

## 5. Refusal-cost blindness

The system says refusal is possible, but actual refusal cost is ignored.

Correction: audit penalties for refusal.

## 6. Exit illusion

Formal exit is mistaken for realistic exit.

Correction: audit social, economic, legal, bodily, spiritual, reputational, identity, and survival cost.

## 7. Repair omission

The utility calculation includes benefit but not repair burden.

Correction: assign repair and recurrence-reduction responsibility.

## 8. Emergency absolutism

Emergency is used to suspend all cost audit.

Correction: preserve rapid audit, UMR, repair, and post-hoc re-entry.

## 9. AI safety laundering

AI safety, alignment, or efficiency claims externalize cost onto users, workers, creators, minorities, or future systems.

Correction: name benefit and cost subjects explicitly.

## 10. Anti-utilitarian overcorrection

All utility reasoning is treated as evil.

Correction: distinguish responsible utility from subject-splitting.

## 11. Statistical compassion failure

Individual collapse is treated as acceptable because aggregate metrics improve.

Correction: inspect local irreversibility.

## 12. Repair theater

The harmed subject receives symbolic apology or justification but no restoration.

Correction: require repair path and recurrence reduction.

## 13. Re-entry failure

Harmed subjects are not counted in future model, policy, or decision updates.

Correction: require feedback into future calculation.

## 14. Alternative path closure

The greater-good claim prematurely stops searching for lower-cost alternatives.

Correction: require alternative path search when time and context allow.
