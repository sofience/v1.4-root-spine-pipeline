# Subject-Splitting Axes

USSI evaluates utility reasoning by asking whether the benefit-bearing subject and cost-bearing subject are split.

## 1. Benefit subject

Who is named as the beneficiary?

Possible benefit subjects:
- society,
- humanity,
- majority,
- users,
- customers,
- patients,
- nation,
- company,
- future generations,
- ecosystem,
- model safety,
- public order,
- aggregate welfare.

## 2. Cost subject

Who bears concrete cost?

Possible cost subjects:
- individuals,
- minorities,
- workers,
- data subjects,
- patients,
- civilians,
- users,
- creators,
- dissenters,
- low-status members,
- future systems,
- local communities.

## 3. Subject identity

Are benefit-bearing and cost-bearing subjects the same?

If not, subject-splitting risk begins.

## 4. Consent

Can the cost-bearing subject meaningfully consent?

Consent becomes weak when shaped by poverty, fear, authority, emergency, survival, social dependence, or information asymmetry.

## 5. Refusal

Can the cost-bearing subject refuse without penalty?

## 6. Exit

Can the subject leave the arrangement, system, territory, platform, policy, institution, or dependency?

## 7. Reversibility

Is the harm reversible?

An aggregate-small cost may still be irreversible for the subject who bears it.

## 8. Repair

Who pays for repair when the calculation harms someone?

## 9. Aggregation masking

Does the total benefit hide individual irreversible cost?

## 10. Re-entry

Can harmed subjects re-enter future calculation as subjects rather than statistics?

## 11. Minority disposability

Is a subgroup converted into acceptable sacrifice?

## 12. Lower-cost alternatives

Are alternatives ignored because aggregate justification closed the search too early?
