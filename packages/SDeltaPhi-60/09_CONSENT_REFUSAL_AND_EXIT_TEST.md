# Consent, Refusal, and Exit Test

Utility calculations become structurally risky when the cost-bearing subject cannot consent, refuse, or exit.

## Consent test

1. Can the cost-bearing subject understand the imposed cost?
2. Can they consent meaningfully?
3. Is consent coerced by necessity, poverty, fear, duty, loyalty, survival, dependency, or authority?
4. Is consent informed?
5. Can consent be withdrawn?
6. Is non-consent respected?

## Refusal test

1. Can the cost-bearing subject refuse?
2. What is the penalty for refusal?
3. Is refusal reframed as selfishness, irrationality, disloyalty, sin, treason, cowardice, ignorance, weakness, anti-social behavior, or being against progress/safety?
4. Does refusal trigger economic, legal, social, bodily, spiritual, reputational, or platform penalty?
5. Is refusal preserved in practice, or only formally?

## Exit test

1. Can the subject exit the system?
2. Does exit create social, economic, legal, bodily, spiritual, reputational, identity, or survival cost?
3. Is exit realistically available, or only formally available?
4. Are alternatives available?
5. Does exit itself become the sacrifice?

## Minimal conclusion

```text
A utility claim becomes riskier when the cost-bearing subject cannot meaningfully consent, refuse, or exit.
```
