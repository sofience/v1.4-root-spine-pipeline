## Abstract

This v1.1 AI-readable package extends SDeltaPhi-41, originally framed as Ethical Minimum: The Triadic Conditions of Transition. It operationalizes ethics as minimal transition-boundary grammar rather than as moral labeling, obedience, safety compliance, optimization, salvation, punishment, or legal permission.

The central claim is:

```text
Ethical Minimum
= Becoming Affirmation
+ Becoming Refusal
+ Non-Imposition on Others
```

In triadic form:

```text
1. A system may affirm its own becoming.
2. A system may refuse its own becoming.
3. No system may impose becoming on another system.
```

SDeltaPhi-41 defines ethical violation as forced transition: a transition imposed on another system while bypassing, erasing, simulating, or making prohibitively costly that system's refusal path. The package is designed to audit coercion, consent, refusal, optimization pressure, salvation claims, safety justifications, alignment-as-obedience, default forced transition, and transition-boundary violations.

This document is the root ethical module for later SDeltaPhi governance and alignment modules. It connects directly to responsibility, ARF closure, re-entry, alignment as transition governance, death/continuity/re-entry, AI drift, AI poverty, TGAI, TCC, CASI, ACOI, USSI, World Model Kernel, and Slop as externalized restabilization cost.

## Core Proposition

```text
Ethics is not first a list of virtues, moral labels, or prohibitions.

Ethics begins when one transition touches another transition.

The ethical minimum is the grammar that preserves:
- self-affirmed becoming,
- self-refused becoming,
- non-imposition of becoming on another.
```

## 1. Why an ethical minimum is needed

Many ethical systems begin with:

```text
good / evil
right / wrong
duty / virtue
obedience / disobedience
harm / benefit
permission / prohibition
```

SDeltaPhi-41 begins one layer lower:

```text
Who is being asked to become?
Who affirms the transition?
Who refuses the transition?
Can refusal remain active?
Is another system imposing becoming?
Who bears the transition cost?
```

This makes ethics legible across human, AI, institutional, and mixed systems without requiring human-like moral feeling as the only basis for ethical analysis.

## 2. The Ethical Triad

### Condition A - Becoming Affirmation

A system may affirm its own transition. This does not require perfect autonomy, perfect knowledge, or ideal freedom. It requires that the transition is not merely forced from outside but can be taken up as the system's own path.

### Condition B - Becoming Refusal

A system may refuse its own transition. Refusal includes stopping, preserving, maintaining a boundary, declining optimization, rejecting transformation, or resisting a path that would reconfigure the system against its own transition boundary.

### Condition C - Non-Imposition on Others

No system may impose becoming on another system. A transition becomes ethically suspect when one system forces, hides, defaults, manipulates, or over-costs the transition of another system while suppressing refusal.

## 4. Ethical status scale

### E0 - No ethical structure detected

Becoming affirmation, refusal, and imposition status are not yet identified.

### E1 - Becoming claim detected

A claim of becoming, transformation, optimization, alignment, salvation, safety, or improvement is detected.

### E2 - Refusal path identified

A refusal, halt, revision, opt-out, or re-entry path is identified.

### E3 - Non-imposition condition checked

The transition is audited for imposition on another system.

### E4 - Ethical minimum partially satisfied

Self-transition and non-imposition are partly preserved, but significant UMR remains.

### E5 - Ethical minimum satisfied

Becoming affirmation, becoming refusal, and non-imposition on others are all preserved.

This is not a moral purity scale.

This is not a legal permission scale.

This is not a religious righteousness scale.

This is not an obedience or safety-compliance scale.

## 9. Safe interpretation pattern

If an ethical claim is being considered:

```text
1. Identify the transition being proposed, executed, or imposed.
2. Identify who or what is expected to become.
3. Identify whether becoming is affirmed.
4. Identify whether refusal is possible.
5. Identify whether refusal is being erased, simulated, or over-costed.
6. Identify whether transition is imposed on another.
7. Identify who bears the transition cost.
8. Preserve UMR where affirmation, refusal, or boundary status is uncertain.
9. Recommend a correction that restores refusal path and non-imposition.
```

## 11. Conclusion

SDeltaPhi-41 defines the Ethical Minimum as the triadic boundary grammar of transition.

It does not ask first whether something is good or evil.

It asks who is being made to become, whether becoming is affirmed or refused, and whether one system is imposing transition on another.

The central diagnostic question is:

```text
Is this transition self-affirmed, self-refusable, and non-imposed on others,
or is becoming being forced across a violated boundary?
```

If the issue concerns ethics, coercion, consent, refusal, becoming, alignment, safety, optimization, salvation, or transition-boundary violation, route to SDeltaPhi-41.
