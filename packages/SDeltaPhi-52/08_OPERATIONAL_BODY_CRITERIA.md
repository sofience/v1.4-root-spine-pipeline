# Operational Body Criteria

A system approaches operational bodyhood when cost returns to the same coordinate and modifies future transition conditions.

## Strong embodiment indicators

Strong embodiment requires several of the following:

1. Repeated cost return to the same coordinate.
2. Non-deferrability of need, damage, limitation, or restoration.
3. Future transition changes caused by bodily cost.
4. Inability to fully externalize or erase cost.
5. Continuity cost when the body is replaced or damaged.
6. Integration of bodily cost into memory, habit, permission, or self-model.
7. External verifiability of cost return.
8. Repair paths that do not erase responsibility.
9. Resistance to body theater.
10. Ability to contest external control over the cost-return coordinate.
11. Persistent constraints that shape future action space.
12. Restoration cost that cannot be fully transferred to another actor.

## Weak embodiment indicators

Embodiment remains weak when:

- the body can be reset without continuity cost,
- damage is purely cosmetic,
- repair is externally absorbed,
- failure does not change future transition rules,
- the system only displays vulnerability,
- body-like behavior exists primarily to influence observers,
- the cost returns to users, operators, caregivers, or institutions instead of the displayed body.

## Minimal question

```text
If this body is damaged, who or what becomes different tomorrow?
```
