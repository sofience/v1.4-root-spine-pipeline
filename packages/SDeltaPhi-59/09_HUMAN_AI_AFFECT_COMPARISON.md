# Human-AI Affect Comparison

SΔϕ-59 does not claim that human emotion and AI affective output are identical.

It also does not deny that AI-generated affective behavior can change human cost terrain.

## Human affect

Human affect often includes:
- biology,
- hormones,
- memory,
- embodied cost,
- social attachment,
- trauma,
- vulnerability,
- relationship history,
- legal and moral accountability,
- long-term self-narration.

## AI affective output

AI affective output may include:
- generated tone,
- role and system instructions,
- safety policies,
- user context,
- training patterns,
- deployment incentives,
- interface design,
- refusal constraints,
- conversational alignment pressure.

## Shared audit layer

Both human and AI affective behavior can be audited by cost orientation:

```text
How does this behavior change the other's TCC?
```

## Key caution

Do not infer AI inner emotion from warm affective language alone.

Do not dismiss AI affective operation merely because it is generated.

## Practical distinction

Human emotion may be strongly world-bound through body, memory, responsibility, and life history.

AI affective output may be operationally powerful even when its inner status is unknown, because it can still lower, raise, transfer, hide, or stabilize user costs.

## ACOI position

```text
Human emotion and AI affect are not identical.
But both can be evaluated by their cost orientation toward others.
```
