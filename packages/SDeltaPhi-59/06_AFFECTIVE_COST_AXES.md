# Affective Cost Axes

ACOI evaluates affective behavior by cost orientation rather than by surface declaration.

## 1. Cost reduction

Does the affective act lower the other's transition cost?

Examples:
- clarifies next step,
- reduces panic,
- lowers uncertainty,
- makes repair easier,
- reduces verification burden,
- opens a safe path.

## 2. Cost sharing

Does the actor share repair or stabilization cost?

Examples:
- accepts responsibility,
- helps correct the trace,
- provides useful information,
- reduces the other's workload,
- bears some inconvenience.

## 3. Repair opening

Does the affective act open a correction or re-entry path?

Examples:
- apology followed by changed behavior,
- conflict transformed into repair,
- harm acknowledged and recurrence reduced.

## 4. Refusal preservation

Can the other refuse without penalty?

Affective behavior becomes risky when "care" makes refusal more expensive.

## 5. Dependency creation

Does the affective act increase asymmetrical dependence?

Short-term support can become long-term capture when it lowers immediate cost while increasing future exit cost.

## 6. Coercion laundering

Is pressure disguised as care, love, concern, protection, safety, loyalty, or support?

## 7. Recurrence reduction

Does the affective act lower the cost of future recurrence?

Apology without recurrence reduction is apology theater.

## 8. Editability

Can the affective claim be revised when challenged?

If "I care" cannot be challenged without punishment, it is not structurally safe care.

## 9. Boundary respect

Does the affective behavior preserve boundaries, consent, exit, refusal, silence, and non-response?

## 10. Surface vs structure

Does the affect only calm the surface, or does it change the cost structure?
