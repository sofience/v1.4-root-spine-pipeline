Abstract
This note proposes a minimal unification of three operations—interpretation, learning,
and inference—within the Sofience–Δϕ framework. The unification claim is
operational: all three are instances of a single pattern, marker re-entry, differing only
by their temporal horizon. Interpretation is one-step re-entry of a path-difference
marker, learning is persistent re-entry that updates the transition law, and inference is
the projection produced by the current law prior to contact with the next transition.
Under this view, error is not a failure signal but the boundary residual between
predicted and realized transition, and becomes the primary input for the next cycle.
1. Notation and minimal primitives
Let φ(t) denote the system state (or a state-distribution) at step t. Let G_t be the
transition law at step t (a kernel, rule, or operator). We distinguish three objects that
are sufficient for the present minimal formulation:

Observed transition: Δφ(t) := φ(t+1) − φ(t) (or distributional change).

Predicted transition: Δφ̂(t) := Ê[φ(t+1) | φ(t), G_t] − φ(t).

Path-difference residual (error candidate): r(t) := Δφ(t) − Δφ̂(t).
The goal is to define minimal operators H_min (interpretation), L_min (learning), and
I_min (inference) and then show they are the same structural move viewed at
different depths.
2. Interpretation as minimal marker re-entry (H_min)

Interpretation (minimal) is the operation that (i) converts a path-difference residual
into a finite marker, and (ii) re-inserts that marker into the next transition. Formally:
m(t) := H_min(r(t))
where m(t) is a discrete or compressed label (a sign, tag, constraint, or boundary
token). Re-entry means that the next-step transition is conditioned by m(t):
φ(t+1) = G_t( φ(t) ; m(t) )
No semantics is required beyond this. H_min is allowed to be lossy; it only needs to
preserve the distinction between paths that should not be treated as equivalent.
3. Learning as persistent re-entry (L_min)
Learning (minimal) occurs when re-entered markers do not merely condition one step,
but update the transition law itself. That is, the system internalizes part of the residual
into its future G.
G_{t+1} := L_min( G_t, m(t), r(t) )
A minimal learning criterion is persistence: there exists k>0 such that the effect of
m(t) is detectable in G_{t+k} even when m(t) is no longer explicitly provided as an
external condition.
Interpretation is therefore one-step re-entry; learning is multi-step re-entry that
changes the rule of re-entry.
4. Inference as pre-contact projection (I_min)
Inference (minimal) is the production of a predicted transition prior to observing Δφ(t).
It is defined as the projection implied by the current law G_t given the current state:
Δφ̂(t) := I_min( φ(t), G_t )
In practical systems, I_min may include a policy that selects an action a(t), but the
minimal requirement is only that a next-step projection exists that can be compared
against the realized transition.
5. Single-structure view: one operator with three
horizons
The three operations can be written as a single recursive cycle:
Δφ̂(t) = I_min(φ(t), G_t)
r(t) = Δφ(t) − Δφ̂(t)
m(t) = H_min(r(t))
G_{t+1} = L_min(G_t, m(t), r(t))

φ(t+1) = G_{t+1}(φ(t) ; m(t))
Interpretation is the H_min step; learning is the L_min step; inference is the I_min
step. They are not separate faculties but phase-shifts of one re-entry mechanism.
6. Where does error belong?
If interpretation–learning–inference are one structure, error is best located at the
coupling boundary between projection and transition. Define a scalarized error signal:
e(t) := d( Δφ(t), Δφ̂(t) )
where d(·,·) may be a norm, a divergence (e.g., KL), or any task-relevant distance. The
key point is that e(t) is generated only after the predicted and realized transitions
meet; it is therefore neither purely ‘inside’ inference nor purely ‘inside’ learning. It is a
boundary residual that becomes r(t), then m(t), then the next update.
In this sense, error is not an object to delete; it is a mark that says: this path cannot
be treated as equivalent to that path. Boundary formation is the operational
after-image of error.
7. Minimal diagnostic implications
The unified view yields a simple diagnostic for model behavior:

Interpretation-only: markers exist (m(t) is produced) but do not persist in G.
System adapts within-step but does not learn.

Learning-dominant: markers persist and G changes, but inference remains weak or
rigid; system becomes stable yet poorly predictive.

Inference-dominant: strong projections exist but markers are not re-entered;
system drifts (high e(t) with low correction).
These regimes align with the ARF discussion: drift, lock-in, and illusion appear when
one horizon dominates and the re-entry cycle fails to close.
